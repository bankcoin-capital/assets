The kStable System


A multi-currency stablecoin family with continuously verifiable backing


Bankcoin Capital — Version 1.0 — 2026-07-07


Contact: admin@bankcoin.capital · Web: bankcoin.capital · Assets & data: assets.bankcoin.capital · Code-verified addresses: github.com/bankcoin-capital/assets



Status vocabulary used throughout this document. Every capability statement carries one of three tags, and the distinction is never blurred:


  • live — deployed on public mainnet today; a verification path (contract address, public API URL, or explorer link) is given so the reader can check the claim without trusting us.
  • operational — built and tested; deployment or activation is in progress. The code exists and its behaviour is specified, but the reader cannot yet exercise it on mainnet.
  • designed — specified in the system's engineering documentation; not yet deployed. Stated so the roadmap is legible, never as a present capability.


    • Abstract


    Almost no stablecoin issuer can *prove* its numbers. Reserve reports are periodic, attestations are snapshots, and the holder is asked to trust a PDF. The kStable system is built on the opposite premise: every money-relevant fact must exist in at least three independently reconstructable views — on-chain aggregate state, an event-sourced server ledger, and a raw event-log replay — and those views must reconcile to the wei, continuously. Disagreement between views is an alarm; matching values are the only acceptable steady state.


    kStables are a family of 38 fiat-referenced tokens (kUSD, kEUR, kJPY, kGBP, kSGD, … through the emerging-market set), each with 6 decimals, live on Base (chain 8453, since 2026-06-12) and Arbitrum One (chain 42161, since 2026-07-02). Every mint at the current contract gate must declare the collateral supporting it — the mint function reverts on an empty backing vector — and the declared backing is recorded in an on-chain registry that anyone with an RPC endpoint can read. Solvency is not asserted; it is simulated: a published six-tier redemption waterfall answers "if everyone redeemed at once, where does the money come from?" per coin and system-wide, and a per-wallet attribution replays each holder's own mint history.


    The same settlement asset is being extended into two adjacent domains: trade finance (letters of credit, electronic bills of lading, and receivables settling in kCurrency in one block instead of days of correspondent banking — contract substrate operational on testnet) and lending (holders borrowing against their own kStables on public money markets — pathway designed; the issuer never pledges its reserve float). This document describes the system as it exists on 2026-07-07, with each capability tagged honestly, and closes with the risk factors an institutional reader should weigh.



    1. System overview


    1.1 What the system is


    Bankcoin Capital issues a family of single-currency stable tokens ("kStables") covering 38 currencies, deployed as upgradeable ERC-20 contracts on public EVM chains. Around the token family sit five subsystems:


    1. A backing and solvency architecture — strict per-mint collateral declaration, an on-chain backing registry, and a published redemption-waterfall simulator (§3).

    2. Market infrastructure — a kUSD-hub network of Uniswap v3 pools per chain, an on-chain FX oracle for the full currency family, and autonomous keepers that recenter liquidity against live FX (§4).

    3. Acquisition and redemption rails — an on-chain auto-convert flow (send USDC/USDT, receive kStable), DEX liquidity, and a capacity-bounded redemption execution engine (§5).

    4. Trade-finance settlement primitives — letters of credit, electronic bills of lading, invoices and receivables that settle in kCurrency (§6).

    5. A lending and collateral pathway — kStables as collateral on public money markets and, longer-term, in bank repo (§7).


    1.2 The accounting constitution


    The system is operated under a set of non-negotiable internal rules — the same discipline a regulated deposit-taking ledger runs under — which we state here because they are the reason the transparency surfaces in §9 can exist at all:


  • Append-only ledgers. No UPDATE, no DELETE on any journal, event log, or money-bearing record. Corrections are new reversing entries that reference the original. Complete history is reconstructable by replaying the log.
  • Double-entry everywhere. Every money-affecting operation — on-chain mint, burn, swap; off-chain fee, credit, payout — has an explicit DR/CR mapping. The general ledger rejects, before commit, any transaction whose legs do not balance, and a full-journal trial-balance walk runs at least hourly.
  • Wei-exactness. Amounts travel as native integer strings end-to-end; floating point never touches a money path. USD-equivalents are derived display values, never primary records.
  • Idempotency on every money-moving endpoint. Client-supplied idempotency keys with server-side replay; a network retry can never move money twice.
  • Three-way reconciliation by construction. For every money-relevant fact the system maintains: (1) the on-chain aggregate (totalSupply(), balanceOf(), registry sums — readable by anyone with an RPC URL); (2) the server's event-sourced Postgres view (append-only event tables plus derived materialised state); (3) the contract event log itself, carrying enough payload that a third party can rebuild both other views from events alone. If any two views agree and the third differs, the discrepancy localises the bug — and any drift above zero wei is an alert and a frozen flow until resolved.

    • The practical consequence for the reader: the numbers this document cites are the numbers you can recompute. Appendix A gives the exact commands.


    1.3 Entities


  • Bankcoin Capital is the issuer of the kStable family and operator of the treasury, FX oracle, keepers, and conversion/redemption engines.
  • Fiat reserves and fiat payment flows (on/off-ramp, customer KYC at the fiat edge) run through regulated banking and payment-institution partners that hold the fiat in custody and provide account-level attestations. Bankcoin Capital is not itself a deposit-taking institution.
  • The affiliated crypto deposit-taking business (CryptoDeposit) is a separate company operated at arm's length; the two do not commingle balance sheets.

    3. Backing and solvency architecture


    This is the centrepiece of the system, and section (1) of what this document exists to explain.


    3.1 Strict portfolio collateralisation — every mint identifies its collateral


    The governing rule, enforced at the token contract layer (live on both chains):


    mintWithBacking(address recipient, uint256 amount, Crypto[] crypto, Fiat[] fiat)
// reverts with EmptyBacking() unless crypto.length != 0 || fiat.length != 0

  • The Crypto struct identifies on-chain collateral by (chainId, asset, amount); the Fiat struct identifies custodial fiat by (bankId, currencyCode, amount) using ISO 4217 numeric codes.
  • It is impossible to mint through this gate without declaring the collateral supporting that mint. The declaration is emitted in the MintWithBacking event — the append-only, publicly replayable record from which per-holder attribution (§3.4) is computed — and accumulated in the on-chain BackingRegistry (sumUsdEquiv, per-slot enumeration; addresses in Appendix B; live).
  • Administrative mints run exclusively through AdminCollateralMinter (live, both chains), a single-entry contract that holds the mint role and forwards to mintWithBacking — so even issuer-initiated supply carries a collateral declaration. Delegated minters (the auto-convert receiver, facility minter, swap engine, bridge receiver) likewise pass the collateral vector they actually hold: USDC pulled in, fiat attested, or source-chain kStables burned.
  • Honest caveat: the legacy mint(to, amount) selector still exists on the token contracts for migration compatibility (role-gated, and scheduled for revocation once every delegated minter is retrofitted). Any use of it is visible on-chain as a mint without a backing declaration — i.e. the escape hatch cannot be exercised invisibly. Verification of collateral *sufficiency and valuation* is by design an off-chain audit function that replays every MintWithBacking event against the contemporaneous FX matrix; the on-chain gate enforces identification, not valuation.
  • A kStable's declared backing is composed of assets *other than itself*; same-coin self-reference has no economic content in the waterfall and contributes nothing to its coverage.

    • 3.2 Capital structure — what the backing is


    The family runs a two-layer portfolio backing model (design parameters, stated as such):


    1. Layer 1 — cross-holdings at par. Each kStable's principal backing is a portfolio of peer instruments and external stable assets recorded in the BackingRegistry, valued through the on-chain FX oracle.

    2. Layer 2 — fiat capital buffer. A fiat-USD capital allocation of 2% of on-issue supply per coin (200 bps) sits above the par layer, giving a per-coin coverage target of 102% with a hard solvency floor of 100%.


    Because the backing basket is not currency-matched one-for-one to each liability, *measured* coverage at live FX breathes around the 102% target (observed system-wide coverage at the Base launch on 2026-06-12: 102.04%, with individual coins oscillating a few tens of basis points either side purely from FX drift). Per-coin live coverage is therefore published as an advisory measure alongside the binding system-wide test:


    > Σ backing (USD-equivalent, at oracle FX) ÷ Σ on-issue liability (USD-equivalent) ≥ 102% target, ≥ 100% floor.


    Treasury bears FX risk symmetrically: a mint adds a backing declaration, a burn releases backing at the oracle FX rate, an in-family swap composes burn + mint with no net change in global supply, and a cross-chain transfer is burn-on-source + mint-on-destination with collateral remaining on the source chain.


    3.3 Three independently reconstructable views


    For the backing and supply facts specifically, the three views are:


    | View | What it is | Who can rebuild it |
|---|---|---|
| **Chain aggregate** | `totalSupply()` / `balanceOf()` per token; `BackingRegistry` slot sums; role membership | Anyone with an RPC URL — no permission, no API key |
| **Server ledger** | Append-only Postgres event tables + derived current-state views; the double-entry GL where every on-chain event posts a journal entry | Bankcoin Capital operationally; auditors on engagement; its *outputs* are public (§9) |
| **Event-log replay** | The `MintWithBacking` / transfer / registry event streams, replayable from deploy block | Any third party listening to the chain alone |

    Reconciliation cadence: continuously in the user interfaces (each solvency page load recomputes and badges chain-vs-server agreement), hourly server-side with alerts on any per-fact drift above zero wei, and a nightly full replay that rebuilds server state from events alone and compares all three ways. The supply API's exclusion registry keeps the *public* circulating number inside the same discipline: it is derived from pinned-block chain reads minus a published address list, so any reader can reproduce it independently (Appendix A).


    3.4 The redemption waterfall — "if everyone redeemed at once, where does it come from?"


    Every kStable publishes its redemption waterfall. The simulator is a pure function — no I/O — over two inputs: the live coverage matrix (from the BackingRegistry) and the FX matrix (from the oracle). Purity matters because it makes the calculation *portable*: the web client can rerun the identical computation against direct chain reads and compare it to the server's answer.


    Six prioritised tiers, consumed in order (design parameters as stated):


    | Tier | Source | FX conversion | Spread haircut |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Native fiat in the redemption currency | none | 0 bps |
| 2 | Same-currency external stablecoins (e.g. USDC for kUSD) | none | 0 bps |
| 3 | Cross-currency external stablecoins | yes | 25 bps |
| 4 | Peer-kStable cross-holdings (burn + FX) | yes | 50 bps |
| 5 | Fiat-USD capital buffer | yes | 25 bps |
| 6 | Cascading liquidation of peer coins' own backing (recursive, bounded at depth 3) | yes | 50 bps |

    The output includes per-tier consumption, the FX path taken, shortfall (if any), and second-order alerts — if redeeming coin A forces liquidation of peer coin B's holdings, the simulator reports B's resulting coverage drop rather than hiding it. A system-stress endpoint runs the full universe at 100% simultaneous redemption and ranks coins weakest-first. Tier 6 exists precisely because the backing is a cross-holding portfolio: we publish the cascade rather than pretending it away (§10, risk 4).


    Per-customer attribution answers the individual holder's version of the question — "what did *I* put in?" — by replaying that wallet's MintWithBacking history in FIFO order. It is served per-wallet behind holder authentication.


    Surfaces: GET /redemption/waterfall/:kStable?demand=<pct>, GET /redemption/system-stress, GET /redemption/attribution/:wallet/:kStable (JWT), and the three-tab customer page at bankcoin.capital/solvency (My kStables / Stress test / System health). The API and page are deployed and publicly reachable; as of 2026-07-07 the public instance at api.bankcoin.capital is the staging deployment serving the Sepolia test-family dataset while the mainnet-data cutover completes — operational (the simulator, tests and endpoints are built and running; the mainnet-fed instance is the deployment in progress). We tag it honestly rather than pointing the reader at a number we would not ourselves rely on.


    3.5 Fiat attestation


    Fiat entries in the BackingRegistry follow a three-layer attestation model (operational as tooling; third-party automation designed): (1) fiat exists at a named custodian bank; (2) the administrative signer verifies custodian balance ≥ cumulative declared backing before signing any fiat-backed mint, archiving dated statement evidence; (3) the declaration lands on-chain in the same transaction as the mint. The roadmap (§11) includes a reserve-oracle phase in which custodian balances are pushed on-chain by the banks themselves and ultimately by independent proof-of-reserve providers, replacing operator attestation with third-party attestation.



    4. Market infrastructure


    4.1 Pool network


    Each chain runs a kUSD-hub star topology on Uniswap v3: one kXXX/kUSD pool for each non-USD kStable in the family, plus stable anchor pools pairing kUSD with external dollar stablecoins. Any cross (kEUR→kCHF) routes through the hub in two legs.


    The flagship anchor is live and verifiable: the kUSD/USDT pool on Base at 0x04105F50C2e00F2dEF96A754EA7cE68170d53fDE — on-chain reads on 2026-07-07 confirm token0 = kUSD (0x6FB0…4D61), token1 = USDT (0xfde4…9bb2), fee tier 100 (0.01%). Pool liquidity across the network as of early July 2026: on the order of $2B on Base and $550M on Arbitrum in protocol-seeded positions (protocol-owned liquidity is excluded from circulating supply, §2). Pool-by-pool addresses are discoverable from the LP-manager contract (Appendix B) and on public DEX indexers.


    4.2 FX oracle


    FxOracleUpgradeable (live: Base 0xfC19c9FbaA9f8391f8E6657dC10e265C1C748Bd5, Arbitrum 0x8f940665B6f09D1c3b10141253c94FaF7698e73b) publishes on-chain rates for the full 38-currency family (37 pairs against USD; crosses synthesised through the USD hub). Its control surface, all on-chain and event-logged:


  • Per-pair staleness windows — consumers can refuse stale rates; isFresh is part of the read interface.
  • Per-push drift caps — operational keeper cap of 500 bps per push against the previous mark, with a contract-level hard ceiling; larger moves require explicit governance action.
  • Per-pair freeze and global pause.
  • Keeper cadence: push on a fixed interval (minutes) or on >50 bps deviation, whichever comes first; rates are sourced from redundant external FX providers and signed by an operator key held in the issuer's hardware-backed KMS.

    • The oracle is self-published by the issuer — we state this plainly rather than obscuring it, and the mitigation roadmap (independent DEX-TWAP deviation guards on any lending-market adapter, and sponsorship of third-party feeds) is in §7 and §10.


    4.3 Keepers and liquidity management


    An autonomous keeper monitors live FX against pool prices and recenters the concentrated-liquidity band of each FX spoke when the mark moves (hourly check plus deviation trigger) — live on both chains. Position management runs through an upgradeable LP-manager contract (Appendix B) so that every liquidity action is an on-chain event, not an off-exchange operation. Cross-chain movement of kStables between Base and Arbitrum uses burn-on-source / mint-on-destination bridge contracts (deployed on both chains) with the relaying service operational.



    5. Acquiring and redeeming kStables


    5.1 Acquiring


  • On-chain convert ("one address per kStable") — live on Base since June 2026: each kStable has a dedicated convert vault address; sending an eligible external stablecoin (USDC, USDT; EURC for kEUR) to the vault triggers a server-verified, KMS-signed fulfilment that delivers the kStable at the oracle rate. Vault addresses are published in the supply-API exclusion registry (they are issuer-controlled and therefore excluded from circulating supply). The same contracts are deployed on Arbitrum with activation in progress — operational there.
  • DEX purchase — live on both chains via the pool network (§4.1).
  • Fiat on-ramp (card / bank rails) — designed; retail fiat purchase is intentionally sequenced *after* redemption execution goes live (below), so the first retail buyer already has a working exit.

    • 5.2 Redemption


    Two distinct things carry the word "redemption", and we keep them separate:


    1. Redemption *transparency* — the waterfall simulator, stress endpoint, and per-wallet attribution of §3.4: built, deployed, publicly queryable (operational, mainnet-data cutover in progress).

    2. Redemption *execution* — actually giving a holder external stablecoins for their kStables. Status: operational — built (code complete and tested, 2026-07-07), not yet enabled in production. We state this without qualification because it is the single most important honesty point in this document: *as of the publication date, holders exit via the DEX pools and convert infrastructure; the direct redemption engine is deployment-in-progress.*


    The redemption execution design (phase 1, "buy back into float"):


  • Holder sends kUSD to the published redemption address; after 6 confirmations the engine pays out USDC/USDT from the market-maker float via a KMS-signed transfer. Received kStables land in issuer inventory (periodic on-chain burn batching is a phase-2 item).
  • Capacity-bounded and fail-closed. Payable capacity is computed from *live chain reads* (float balance and allowance minus committed in-flight payouts) at quote time and immediately before payout; if the RPC is unreadable the quote is refused with an error — never a guessed number. Over-capacity requests are refused up-front with the real capacity in the response, not queued silently.
  • Bounds at launch (design parameters): minimum $1, maximum $250,000 per transaction, redemption fee 0 bps.
  • Every flow is an append-only state machine (quoted → inflow_seen → inflow_confirmed → payout_sent → settled, with explicit stuck_* states that alert operations); execution requires an idempotency key; a paid inflow can never be silently dropped.

    • 5.3 Who can transact — the entries-gated / exits-open doctrine


    Geographic gating applies to entry surfaces only (the issuer's web applications and primary issuance are unavailable in the EU/EEA pending MiCA authorisation, in a small set of other jurisdictions by policy, and in comprehensively sanctioned jurisdictions). Exits are never geo-gated: kStables are permissionless ERC-20s acquired on open DEXes, so refusing redemption by geography would be the failure mode, not a control. The redemption API is globally reachable by design. Jurisdictional control on the payout leg is person-level sanctions screening — a screening hit (or an unavailable screening service) holds the payout fail-closed for review; it is never a geographic confiscation (§8).



    6. Trade finance — kStables as the settlement layer


    Section (2) of the owner's brief: what the settlement asset is *for*.


    6.1 The problem being priced


    International trade settlement today runs on correspondent banking (days, fees, reconciliation breaks), paper documents whose integrity is enforced by courier ("three originals" of a bill of lading), and document examination with first-presentation discrepancy rates near 70% under UCP strict compliance. The historical failure record — LC counterparty collapses, forged bills of lading (industry fraud estimates in the billions annually), and supply-chain-finance blow-ups built on financing *unconfirmed future receivables* — is a record of information asymmetry and settlement lag, not of credit analysis being impossible.


    6.2 What has been built


    A trade-finance contract substrate settling in kCurrency — operational: built, tested (~141 dedicated tests within a 603-test contract suite as of 2026-05-19), and deployed to testnet; mainnet deployment is gated behind the external security audit (§11). The substrate:


    | Contract | Function |
|---|---|
| `LetterOfCreditUpgradeable` | UCP 600 / eUCP 2.0-mapped documentary credit: applicant's kCurrency is encumbered non-custodially via the token lock primitive; drawing is atomic when all documentary conditions attest |
| `EBLRegistryUpgradeable` | DCSA-conformant electronic bill of lading as ERC-721 — singularity, integrity (payload hash), and exclusive control by construction, mapped to the MLETR reliability criteria |
| `InvoiceRegistryUpgradeable` + `ReceivableTokenUpgradeable` | Invoice (mutable commercial document with a full dispute/amendment state machine) strictly separated from receivable (immutable ERC-721 financial instrument) |
| `ConditionPrecedentUpgradeable` | Generalised gating: AllOf/AnyOf condition groups with authorised attestors — reused by LC drawing, eBL minting, receivable minting |
| `TradeEventLogUpgradeable` | Append-only typed event log (~80 well-known codes from quote through customs, insurance, eBL and compliance) |
| Supporting set | Party-role management (M-of-N, senior-override), dispute registry with bond economics, event-driven margin facility, ordered payment waterfall, competitive financing marketplace, trade-registry root object |

    Two design points deserve emphasis for an institutional reader:


  • The Greensill invariant. A receivable token *cannot exist* for an invoice that has not crossed the irrevocability gate — not via admin override, not via emergency action. Financing unconfirmed future receivables, the headline failure mode of 2021-era supply-chain finance, is structurally impossible at the contract layer rather than discouraged by process.
  • Settlement finality in one block. When the final documentary condition attests, the LC draw executes atomically: the encumbered kCurrency moves from applicant to beneficiary in the same transaction, with no correspondent-banking leg and no discrepancy re-examination cycle. Cross-currency settlement composes with the atomic swap engine (kEUR in, kSGD out, same block) — payment-versus-payment with no temporal gap, which is the definition of eliminating Herstatt risk. This is what "kStables as the settlement layer" concretely means: the 38-currency family makes the *payment leg* of an LC, an eBL endorsement, or a receivable purchase a same-second, multi-currency primitive.

    • 6.3 Standards posture


    The contract surface is mapped article-by-article against the governing standards rather than gesturing at them: UCP 600 / eUCP 2.0 (documentary credits), URC 522 (collections), ISP98 / URDG 758 (standby instruments — designed, a variant of the shipped LC), DCSA / BIMCO / FIATA data models and MLETR reliability criteria (eBL), ISO 20022 tsin/tsrv message emission via a gateway (designed), and the cross-cutting ISO identifier stack (BIC, LEI, ISO 4217/3166/8601) plus EIP-712/EIP-1271 signing. The internal coverage matrix flags eight known gaps (e.g. UCP Art. 16 single-notice discrepancy workflow, pseudonymous-wallet holder identification under MLETR, P&I club approval for a natively chain-issued eBL) with remediation plans — we prefer a documented gap to an implied completeness.


    Status summary for this section: the settlement substrate is operational (testnet); productisation — carrier, customs, insurer oracle integrations, the SWIFT/ISO 20022 gateway, and pilot programmes — is designed. No trade-finance revenue claim is made in this document.



    7. Lending and collateral


    Section (3) of the owner's brief.


    7.1 The governance principle, stated first


    Customers borrow against their own kStable holdings. The issuer does not pledge its reserve float or backing assets as collateral, ever. Lending demand is a *use* of the instrument by its holders; the reserves stay segregated and unencumbered behind the liability they back. Any yield-bearing wrapper (below) is a separate, separately-capitalised structure — never a lien on the payment coin's reserves. We state this as a governance commitment because the failure pattern it excludes (issuer rehypothecating reserves into its own leverage) is the one that turns a liquidity event into a solvency event.


    7.2 Money-market pathway (designed)


    The pathway to kStables as accepted DeFi collateral is documented and sequenced, and is tagged designed in its entirety — none of it is live today:


  • Permissionless venues first: Curve stableswap pools and Morpho Blue markets (kUSD collateral / USDC loan and inverted) on Base and Arbitrum. An oracle adapter for Morpho has been written and tested but not deployed.
  • Oracle honesty is the gating engineering item. Money markets need a Chainlink-interface price feed. The planned adapter wraps the FX oracle with an independent DEX-TWAP deviation guard — if the market price of kUSD deviates from par beyond a threshold, the adapter reverts and liquidations halt rather than misprice. In parallel, the roadmap commits to sponsoring independent third-party feeds (Chainlink/RedStone/Pyth class) for the hub coins.
  • Governance venues after track record: an Aave v3 listing proposal with deliberately conservative initial parameters (proposed LTV 75%, liquidation threshold 80%, initial supply cap $20M — design parameters for a governance proposal, not live terms), preceded by ≥90 days of public reconciliation history, the external audit, and the multisig/timelock migration. Comparable proposals to Compound/Spark follow the same pack.

    • 7.3 Bank-grade collateral ladder (designed)


    For regulated balance sheets, the documented progression is: bilateral GMRA-style repo between willing banks → tri-party agent eligibility → HQLA recognition under liquidity-coverage rules (initially Level 2B) → committed standby repo facilities → central-bank eligibility where realistic. Alongside it sits a multilateral netting design in which the protocol coordinates daily net settlement of inter-bank kCurrency obligations with atomic on-chain settlement. Every rung of this ladder is a target state with named prerequisites (audits, legal opinions, secondary-market depth), not a present capability, and this document makes no Basel-classification claim on behalf of any regulator.


    7.4 Business model


    The issuer's economics are the boring kind, by intent:


  • Income = reserve yield + fees. The fiat and stable-asset reserves earn short-duration market yield; the protocol charges conversion/redemption fees (currently parameterised at 0 bps for redemption phase 1) and, in the trade/netting designs, basis-point service fees.
  • Holders get a fully-reserved instrument, not a yield product. The payment coin pays no interest to holders — consistent with the direction of the major regulatory regimes (US GENIUS Act §4(a)(11); MiCA Art. 50; MAS's stablecoin framework). Where a yield-bearing *wrapper* is offered in future, it will be a separate legal entity, a disclosed investment product, geofenced away from jurisdictions where it cannot lawfully be offered — designed, and explicitly gated on counsel sign-off before any code ships.

    • This document contains no yield promise, no APY projection, and no forward-looking return statement, and none should be inferred.



    8. Compliance and controls


  • Travel rule (live at the registry layer). Every kStable on every chain has a policy registered in the on-chain TravelRuleRegistry: transfers below US$1,000,000 equivalent (per-currency thresholds fixed in native units at a published FX snapshot) flow freely; transfers at or above the threshold require preclearance by the ordering role, consistent with FATF Recommendation 16. Enforcement executes in the token's transfer hook — bound family-wide on Arbitrum, binding completing on a subset of Base tokens (§2).
  • KYC and sanctions at the money edges, person-level. Identity obligations attach where fiat enters or leaves (partner-bank KYC at on-ramp; sanctions screening on redemption payouts). Screening is person-level and fail-closed — a hit or an unavailable screening service holds the individual payout for review. It is never implemented as geographic confiscation of tokens: the entries-gated / exits-open doctrine of §5.3 is a policy invariant.
  • Token-layer controls, disclosed. The token contracts carry pause, per-address freeze, and law-enforcement seize capabilities behind admin roles, plus a compliance registry consulted by transfer hooks. We disclose these plainly: institutions should know both that the controls exist (their regulators will ask) and that their use is governed by the append-only audit discipline of §1.2 — every invocation is an on-chain event.
  • Entity separation. Issuance (Bankcoin Capital) and the affiliated crypto deposit-taking business are separate companies at arm's length; fiat custody sits with regulated partners (§1.3).
  • Data minimisation. No personal data lives on-chain; attribution queries (§3.4) are authenticated per-wallet; this document and the public asset repositories carry no personal names by policy.

    10. Risk factors


    An institutional reader should weigh the following. We list them because a transparency-first system that hid its risks would be incoherent.


    1. Smart-contract and upgrade-control risk. All core contracts are UUPS-upgradeable. As of this document's date, upgrade and admin authority is held by a hardware-wallet-controlled issuer EOA; migration to a multi-signature Safe with an enforced timelock is planned but not yet executed. No completed external security audit exists yet for the kStable family; an audit engagement is budgeted and is a stated precondition for the mainnet deployment of the trade-finance substrate and for governance-venue listing proposals. Until both land, holders are exposed to issuer key compromise and to contract defects.

    2. Oracle risk. FX rates are published by the issuer's own keeper. On-chain guardrails (staleness windows, drift caps, freeze/pause, full event trail) bound the damage of a bad push but do not make the feed independent. Mitigations (DEX-TWAP deviation guards on lending adapters; third-party feed sponsorship) are roadmap items, not present facts.

    3. Liquidity and redemption-capacity risk. Direct redemption execution is not yet enabled (§5.2); when enabled it is deliberately capacity-bounded by the funded float and per-transaction caps, and exits meanwhile depend on DEX liquidity that is substantially protocol-seeded. A holder wishing to exit a very large position quickly may face price impact or capacity waits. We publish capacity honestly rather than promising unbounded at-par exit.

    4. Backing-structure (cross-holding) risk. The backing model is a portfolio: peer-kStable cross-holdings at par plus a 2% fiat capital layer, targeting 102% coverage with a 100% floor — it is not a 1:1 segregated same-currency fiat reserve per coin, and this document should not be read as claiming that model. Correlated redemptions propagate through the cross-holding graph; the waterfall's tier-6 cascade (bounded at depth 3) exists to *measure* that propagation, and the published stress endpoint ranks the weakest coins first. Measured coverage additionally breathes with FX because the basket is not currency-matched per liability.

    5. Counterparty and custody risk. Fiat backing sits with a small number of regulated banking partners; signing infrastructure is a self-hosted KMS operated by the issuer. Failure, freeze, or insolvency at a custodian, or compromise of the KMS, would impair the corresponding backing slots or operations.

    6. Regulatory risk. The family is not authorised under MiCA (EU/EEA entry surfaces are gated accordingly); stablecoin regimes in the US, EU, Singapore and elsewhere are moving, and future rules could constrain issuance, distribution, redemption mechanics, or the planned yield-wrapper structure in any jurisdiction. Statements of entity structure in this document are descriptions of fact, not legal conclusions.
